# Extraction Matrix

Use this reference when building a target journal profile from representative papers.

## Paper Metadata

For each paper, record:

- Journal name.
- Title.
- Author(s).
- Year and issue.
- Article type.
- Field or subfield.
- Method.
- Empirical material.
- Core concept.
- Section structure.
- Topic proximity to the user's manuscript.

## Coding Dimensions

### Research Problem

Identify the initial problem source:

- Real-world governance problem.
- Policy change or institutional reform.
- Theoretical controversy.
- Conceptual ambiguity.
- Empirical contradiction.
- Methodological gap.
- Historical puzzle.
- Case anomaly.

Then identify how the article narrows the problem into a research question. Look for moves such as contradiction sharpening, scope narrowing, mechanism missingness, concept redefinition, or empirical puzzle construction.

### Abstract

Code the abstract sequence:

- Starting point: background, theory gap, empirical puzzle, policy context, direct question.
- Method or material presentation.
- Finding form: mechanism chain, typology, causal result, historical stage, institutional logic, conceptual refinement, policy implication.
- Contribution claim: theory, concept, method, evidence, policy, or mixed.
- Degree of restraint in claims.

### Introduction

Code how the introduction moves:

- From broad phenomenon to academic question.
- From policy importance to theoretical problem.
- From existing explanation to unresolved puzzle.
- From case detail to general mechanism.
- From concept to empirical stakes.

Identify whether the journal prefers phenomenon-first, policy-first, theory-first, contradiction-first, history-first, or case-first openings.

### Literature Review

Record the organizing principle:

- Schools or debates.
- Concept lineage.
- Mechanism categories.
- Research fields.
- Methodological approaches.
- Historical stages.
- Analytical dimensions.

Assess how the review creates room for contribution. Prefer labels such as explanatory gap, concept gap, mechanism gap, evidence gap, scope gap, or integration gap. Avoid accepting vague claims that "existing research is insufficient" as a real gap.

### Concepts and Theory

Identify:

- Where the core concept first appears.
- Whether it is defined directly, reconstructed through literature, developed through empirical material, or refined after analysis.
- Whether the article builds a framework, adjusts a concept, proposes a mechanism, integrates theories, or compares perspectives.
- How concept hierarchy is arranged: main concept, dimensions, mechanisms, variables, actors, processes.

### Evidence and Empirical Material

Classify how material enters the argument:

- Process tracing.
- Comparative case analysis.
- Statistical identification.
- Policy text analysis.
- Interview-based interpretation.
- Archival reconstruction.
- Historical narrative.
- Mixed evidence.

Then code what evidence does: prove causal effect, reconstruct process, support mechanism, reveal institutional logic, demonstrate typology, explain historical evolution, or refine concept.

### Mechanism Explanation

A mechanism is not a factor list. Code whether the paper shows how one condition leads to another through:

- Actors.
- Institutions.
- Authority.
- Incentives.
- Resources.
- Information.
- Rules.
- Meaning.
- Networks.
- Interaction.
- Temporal sequence.

Identify the mechanism form: sequential, layered, interactive, typological, historical, feedback-loop, or comparative.

### Conclusion

Record how the conclusion returns to the research question:

- Restates findings.
- Names theoretical contribution.
- Refines a concept.
- Explains policy implication.
- States empirical boundary.
- Identifies future research.

Assess claim control: whether it avoids policy slogans, overgeneralization, unsupported prediction, or claims beyond evidence.

## Cross-Paper Synthesis Rules

Do not infer journal style from one article. Mark a pattern as journal-level only when at least one of these is true:

- It recurs across several papers by different authors.
- It appears in different methods or topics within the corpus.
- It is central to multiple accepted article structures.
- It is strongly reinforced by editorial framing, special column conventions, or repeated section architecture.

When patterns conflict, separate them by article type or method. For example: "case-based articles prefer process reconstruction, while quantitative articles present contribution through identification plus mechanism tests."

## Evidence Standards for Claims

Every profile claim should be supported by corpus evidence in one of these forms:

- Pattern frequency across the corpus.
- Repeated structural sequence.
- Repeated problematization route.
- Repeated relation among concept, evidence, and theory.
- Repeated conclusion boundary.

Avoid unsupported labels such as "rigorous," "high-level," "deep," or "strongly academic." Replace them with observable writing tendencies.
